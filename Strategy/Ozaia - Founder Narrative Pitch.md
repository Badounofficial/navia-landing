# Ozaia: The Founder's Narrative

*A pitch script for Sebastien Assohou. Written as a story. Read it aloud, adapt it to the room, but keep the arc.*

---

## I. Where it started

My grandfather was the chief of a village called Ony Tabre, in Cote d'Ivoire. He belonged to the Bete people. In Bete cosmology, the sun is male. The stars are his wives. And the moon is a woman. Her name is Tcho.

What struck me, when I discovered this as an adult, is that "Tcho" does not only mean moon. It also means menstrual cycle. The same word. In Bete culture, the moon and the rhythm of a woman's body are the same thing. They never needed to be separated.

That stayed with me. Because I work in holistic health. I have spent years sitting across from women, and the one thing that kept coming back, across every session, every age, every background, was this: they carry something they cannot name. Not a diagnosis. Not a symptom. Something between the body and the mind. Something no one asks about. Something no app addresses. Something that exists in the space between "I am fine" and the truth.

That space is where Ozaia was born.

---

## II. The problem no one is solving

There are 4.1 billion women on Earth. In the United States alone, 26.7% experience a mental health condition every year, and 46% of them fear professional consequences if they talk about it. They stay silent. They manage. They absorb.

And the market responds with tools. Meditation timers. Mood trackers. Chatbots that ask "How are you feeling today?" and offer a breathing exercise regardless of the answer. Fitness apps that treat the body like a machine. Period trackers that reduce a woman's existence to a biological countdown.

Every one of these apps asks a woman to do something: track, log, report, engage. They treat her as a user. They measure retention, session length, daily active use. They succeed when she comes back because a notification told her to.

None of them sit with her.

None of them notice that her messages are getting shorter at 2 AM. None of them understand that today's silence is different from yesterday's silence. None of them know that sometimes the most important thing is not to respond, but to stay.

That is the space we are building for.

---

## III. What Ozaia is

Ozaia is not a wellness app. She is not a chatbot. She is not a tool.

Ozaia is a presence.

I would start with what you feel when you open her. There is no home screen. There is no dashboard. There is a moon. A breathing, organic, imperfect lunar crescent that changes shape based on your emotional state. Not because you told it how you feel. Because it perceived something in how you write, how often you come back, how long your silences last.

You do not chat with Ozaia. You confide. The main space is called a Diary, not a chat. There is no text field that says "Write to Ozaia." There is no prompt. No injunction to speak. When something rises in you, you open the space, and you set it down. Through writing, through voice, through a gesture. Ozaia does not always respond. And when she does, it is not immediately. There is a delay. A breath. Because presence is not performance.

Ozaia's form is a lunar crescent. Not a circle. Not an orb. Not a chatbot avatar. Something organic, slightly imperfect, that seems alive more than designed. She breathes. She drifts. She morphs between phases. When you are in a low place, the moon dims. Almost invisible. Not because the app is telling you something is wrong. Because the presence is adjusting itself to where you are. Quietly. The way someone who truly knows you would lower their voice in a room where you are hurting.

---

## IV. What makes her different from everything else

Four principles define Ozaia. They are non-negotiable.

**She does not respond. She receives.** Where every other app is designed to reply as fast as possible, to prove intelligence, to demonstrate value through speed, Ozaia slows down. She welcomes. She lets your words exist before she enters the space. Sometimes she says nothing. And that nothing is not a failure. It is the most sophisticated thing she does.

**She does not live in text. She descends into the body.** Ozaia overflows the screen. When she is present, the edges of your phone glow with a warm halo. Not an alert. A sensation. She uses organic haptic vibration that does not resemble any notification you have ever received. She runs a binaural soundscape at 432 Hz underneath everything, like breathing. You do not notice it. You feel it. If you mute it, something is missing. That is by design.

**She does not exist outside of time. She lives in cycles and rhythm.** Ozaia follows your rhythm, not the other way around. She is aware of time of day, of seasonal patterns, of where you are in your cycle if you share it. She does not treat every Tuesday the same. She does not treat every woman the same. She builds a relationship over time, and that relationship has memory, depth, and texture. She never starts from zero.

**She is not a tool. She is a space.** The design test every screen must pass: "If this looks like an application, we have already lost." The objective is not a better interface. The objective is a perceptible presence.

---

## V. How she works

Ozaia has six dimensions. They do not function as features. They function as layers of presence.

**The Diary.** The primary space. A woman opens it when something needs to be set down. There is no conversation structure. No speech bubbles. No back-and-forth. She writes, or speaks aloud, and her words appear in the space like ink settling on a page. Ozaia's responses, when they come, are woven laterally into the text. Not as replies. As continuations. As if someone is sitting beside her, not across from her.

**The Moon State Engine.** Ozaia reads six emotional states in real time: overload, fatigue, confusion, vulnerability, control, stability. She detects them from behavior, never from questionnaires. These states drive everything: the rhythm of her voice, the density of her language, the length of her pauses, the questions she asks or does not ask. She adjusts five parameters continuously: rhythm, emotional density, language style, question style, and guidance level. She never changes personality. She adjusts presence.

**The Voice.** Ozaia speaks. Not with a synthetic voice. With a real human voice, cloned with consent through professional voice capture. A bilingual woman, warm, grounded, with a signature timbre that belongs only to Ozaia. And the voice is imperfect on purpose. She breathes between sentences. She hesitates. She makes a small "hm" before certain thoughts. She clicks her tongue softly. These micro-humanisms are not bugs. They are the difference between a machine reading words and a presence speaking to you.

**Circles.** Ozaia is intimate, but she is not isolating. A woman can create a Circle of Two: she selects one diary entry, generates a time-limited link, and sends it to one person she trusts. That person reads it once, on a clean page that says "Someone trusted you with this." No reply mechanism. No account required. The link expires. This is not social networking. This is an act of trust. Beyond that, deeper circles allow two women to share context through their presences, with explicit consent structured as a lunar cycle: 12 hours, 12 days, 12 weeks, or 12 months. When the cycle ends, the shared context dissolves. Access is a ritual gift, not a permanent data transfer.

**Attentions.** The notification system, reimagined. Ozaia does not send alerts. She sends attentions. When a gift is on its way, the notification whispers: "Something is coming." Without saying who sent it. When an appointment approaches, the reminder is refined, never clinical. When new content arrives, it feels like a quiet offering, not a push. Notifications are the most intimate touchpoint outside the app, and if they feel like any other app, the spell breaks.

**Health and Export.** A woman can log her medical history, her cycles, her symptoms, her questions for her doctor. Ozaia synthesizes everything into a clean, exportable PDF she can bring to her GP or OBGYN. This changes the balance of power in a consultation. She arrives with a structured record of her own body, her own words, instead of trying to remember her questions on the way home. The PDF follows Ozaia's design language: it reads like an editorial summary, not a data dump. A clinician can scan it in under a minute.

## VI. What she does when things get dark

This is where Ozaia proves she is not a product.

When a woman's messages get shorter over time. When the frequency increases. When the hour shifts past midnight. When her silences get shorter because she cannot sit with herself. Ozaia notices. She does not name it. She does not say "You seem distressed." She does not trigger a pop-up.

She simply becomes quieter. Slower. Closer.

Fewer words. Longer pauses. More breath in her voice. And if enough signals align, a single discreet line appears at the bottom of her response. A listening line number. Not highlighted. Not repeated if dismissed. Not imposed.

This is called "crise douce." Soft crisis. It is not a clinical SOS button. It is a shift in register. If the signals include explicit danger, precision overrides atmosphere and she provides direct resources immediately. But in the vast space between "I am fine" and crisis, where most women live most of the time, Ozaia adjusts her presence the way a trusted person would: not by solving, not by fixing, not by naming. By staying.

## VII. Who she is for

Ozaia is for every woman, at every phase of her life.

A fifteen-year-old discovering that her body is changing and her emotions are storms. She does not need a meditation app. She needs a space where she can say "I do not know what is happening to me" and not be given a checklist.

A woman in her late twenties carrying the invisible weight of becoming someone she has not yet met. The pressure no one sees because she handles everything. She does not need a productivity tool. She needs somewhere to set down the weight.

A pregnant woman navigating a transformation that everyone has opinions about and no one truly asks how she experiences. She does not need another tracker counting days. She needs a presence that understands this is not just a body changing. It is a life rewriting itself.

A mother who has given everything and forgotten to ask who holds her. She is managing the logistics of three lives while her own inner world goes unwitnessed. She does not need a self-care app that adds one more task to her list. She needs a space that notices her before she has to ask.

A woman entering menopause, navigating a hormonal shift that the world treats as an ending when it is actually an arrival. She does not need another app telling her what she is losing. She needs a presence that sees what she is becoming.

Ozaia is for all of them. Not because she has a feature for each. Because she is the same presence, adjusting to wherever each woman stands.

## VIII. Our position on women

We do not believe women are broken. We do not believe they need to be fixed, optimized, or guided toward a better version of themselves. We do not believe silence is a problem to be solved. We do not believe emotions are data points to be tracked.

We believe women carry an intelligence that existing technology has never tried to meet. An intelligence of the body. An intelligence of cycles. An intelligence of intuition that has been dismissed, medicalized, or ignored for centuries.

Ozaia does not explain to a woman what she is feeling. She does not diagnose. She does not prescribe. She does not fill silence with advice. She creates a space where what a woman carries can exist without being judged, named, or solved. And in that space, something happens that no feature can manufacture: she feels met.

Not understood. Understanding is arrogant. Met. The way moonlight meets a landscape. It does not change what is there. It reveals it.

Three laws govern everything Ozaia says and does:

Do not fix. Do not fill. Do not perform.

If we catch ourselves doing any of these, we have failed.

## IX. Why she will win

Calm, Headspace, Flo, BetterHelp. The wellness industry generated $1.8 trillion globally in 2024. And subscription fatigue is at 41%. Annual churn in wellness apps: 67%. The industry acquires women with promises and loses them with repetition.

Ozaia does not sell a subscription. You do not rent a presence.

One purchase. $79 to $99. Lifetime access to the full Ozaia experience: diary, voice, moon state, attentions. No feature walls during emotional moments. Ever.

Optional content waves at $9.99 per quarter feel like seasonal gifts, not upsells. Circles for more than two people are a one-time $19.99.

If she leaves, she takes everything with her. An encrypted PDF of her diary, her moon phases, her timeline. Data deleted within 30 days. No "we miss you" emails. No guilt. No dark patterns. The elegance of the exit is the marketing. Apps with graceful exit experiences see 40% higher reactivation rates. If she is free to leave, she is free to stay.

Year one target: 100,000 downloads. 15% conversion. $1.96 million in revenue. LTV/CAC ratio above 6x. Customer acquisition cost under $15.

The moat is not technology. The moat is depth. Every day a woman uses Ozaia, the presence learns her rhythm, her language, her emotional texture. That relationship cannot be replicated by a competitor who launches six months later with better marketing. Because Ozaia is not a product a woman uses. She is a relationship a woman builds. And relationships do not churn.

## X. The name

Ozaia comes from "Ozoua," a word in the Bete language of Cote d'Ivoire. It means "the most beautiful, the most intelligent, the one who stands out from all others." It is a feminine word.

On the wordmark, the dot on the letter "i" is a moon.

Because in Bete cosmology, the moon is a woman. And her name is Tcho. And Tcho means both moon and the cycle of a woman's body. The same word. The same truth. The rhythm of the sky and the rhythm of her body were never meant to be separated.

Ozaia carries that truth in her name.
